Replicating Card & Krueger (1994)

Minimum Wages and Employment in New Jersey and Pennsylvania

A concise replication of key difference-in-differences findings from Card and Krueger (1994).
Author

Stan He

Published

April 22, 2026

Introduction

In Minimum Wages and Employment: A Case Study of the Fast-Food Industry in New Jersey and Pennsylvania, Card and Krueger (1994) study whether New Jersey’s April 1992 minimum-wage increase from $4.25 to $5.05 reduced employment in fast-food restaurants. Their core design compares stores in New Jersey to similar stores in eastern Pennsylvania, where the minimum wage did not change over the same period.

This paper is a classic empirical challenge to the standard competitive prediction that a binding minimum wage must reduce employment. Its identification strategy is a simple but powerful difference-in-differences design: compare employment before and after the policy in New Jersey, then subtract the same before-after change in Pennsylvania.

In this post, I replicate a key employment result from the paper, reproduce part of the main regression table, and add one extra comparison showing why the difference-in-differences logic matters more than a naive cross-sectional comparison.

What the Authors Are Studying

The causal question is straightforward:

Did New Jersey’s 1992 minimum-wage increase reduce fast-food employment relative to a comparable control group?

The treatment group is fast-food restaurants in New Jersey. The control group is fast-food restaurants in eastern Pennsylvania. The main outcome is full-time-equivalent employment (FTE) per store.

Data and Setup

The public replication files contain a flat-file dataset with 410 observations, along with a codebook and a SAS checking script. The codebook shows the original variables used in the article, including first- and second-wave employment counts, starting wages, ownership status, region indicators, store chain, and several price variables.

Replication 1: Part of Table 2

A useful first check is whether the data line up with the descriptive comparisons in Table 2. Below I reproduce selected means by state for wave 1 and wave 2.

These values line up closely with the paper’s descriptive message: Pennsylvania stores started with somewhat higher average employment, but New Jersey experienced a much larger rise in starting wages after the law change. That confirms the treatment was real and economically meaningful.

Replication 2: Table 3 Difference-in-Differences

The paper’s main result is in Table 3. The key comparison is the change in FTE employment in New Jersey relative to the change in Pennsylvania.

State FTE Before FTE After Change
PA 23.33 21.17 -2.28
NJ 20.44 21.03 0.47
NJ - PA -2.89 -0.14 2.75

Interpretation

My replicated difference-in-differences estimate is about 2.75 FTE employees, very close to the paper’s reported relative gain of 2.76. That means employment in New Jersey increased relative to Pennsylvania after the minimum-wage hike, rather than falling.

This is the central empirical surprise of the paper. A textbook competitive model would predict a negative effect. Card and Krueger instead find no evidence of employment loss in this setting.

My replicated estimates closely match the published point estimates, so the core empirical conclusion is successfully reproduced.

Replication 3: Table 4 Regression Results

The paper also estimates reduced-form regressions for the change in employment. I reproduce the core versions below using the same derived variables as the SAS check file.

Model Coefficient Standard Error N
(i) demp ~ nj 2.71 1.20 375
(ii) demp ~ nj + controls 2.75 1.20 375
(iii) demp ~ gap 16.14 6.05 360
(iv) demp ~ gap + controls 15.63 6.16 360
(v) demp ~ gap + region controls 12.03 7.39 360

Interpretation

These estimates closely track the paper’s Table 4:

  • the New Jersey dummy specification gives a positive coefficient of about 2.7 to 2.8;
  • the gap specification gives a positive coefficient around 15 to 16 in the simpler models;
  • adding controls does not reverse the result.

So the regression-adjusted version tells the same basic story as the raw difference-in-differences: I find no evidence that the employment effect is negative.

My replicated estimates closely match the published point estimates, so the core empirical conclusion is successfully reproduced.

Additional Analysis: Naive Comparison vs. Difference-in-Differences

For the extra piece, I compare a naive post-period cross-state comparison with the actual difference-in-differences estimate.

Estimate Value
Pre-period NJ - PA difference -2.89
Post-period NJ - PA difference -0.14
Difference-in-differences 2.75

Why this matters

A naive post-treatment comparison is not causal because New Jersey and Pennsylvania were already different before treatment. In the raw data, New Jersey stores started smaller on average. Difference-in-differences corrects for that baseline gap by comparing changes, not just levels.

This comparison shows why a post-period cross-sectional gap is not a causal estimate: New Jersey stores already differed from Pennsylvania stores before the policy.

That is exactly why this paper became such a famous applied example. Its result is not driven by a simple treated-versus-control comparison, but by a quasi-experimental before-after design with a nearby control group.

Conclusion

This replication reproduces the main empirical result of Card and Krueger (1994): I find no evidence that New Jersey’s 1992 minimum-wage increase reduced fast-food employment relative to Pennsylvania. My replicated difference-in-differences estimate is very close to the published result, and the regression specifications tell the same story.

The most important lesson is methodological as much as substantive. If I only looked at post-treatment differences, I would miss the causal logic. The value of the paper comes from the design: compare New Jersey to a plausible control group, compare before to after, and focus on the relative change.

That is also why this paper fits so well into the broader causal inference framework from class. It is a clean, intuitive example of how a simple quasi-experimental design can overturn a strong theoretical prior.
